Part 1

Three Shifts
Happening Right Now

Shift 1: Data Entry → Data Direction

Before

Bookkeepers enter transactions.
Marketing assistants compile reports.
Admins schedule and organize.

→

Now

AI handles the rote work.
The human decides what questions to ask, what data matters, and what to do with the answer.

Monday morning, before AI

Marketing intern spends 3 hours researching 5 competitors — copying websites, pulling pricing, building a spreadsheet, writing a summary.

→
Monday morning, with AI

AI drafts the analysis in 5 minutes. Intern spends 3 hours on strategy, client calls, and catching what AI got wrong.

Shift 2: Specialists → T-Shaped Builders

The most in-demand roles now combine domain knowledge + AI fluency:

AI-Augmented Marketing Analyst

Uses AI for customer segmentation — writes the strategy

Business Process Automation Specialist

Maps workflows — directs AI to automate them

AI Product Manager

Doesn't code — shapes what AI products do

Students don't need to code. They need to describe what they want built clearly. That's context engineering — and it's a business skill.

Shift 3: Consuming Content → Building Tools

Old Model

Learn software:
Excel, QuickBooks, Canva

Students use tools others built.

→

New Model

Build the tool you need using AI:
Customer intake form, inventory tracker, marketing dashboard

Students create tools.

Real example: One of my students got tired of guessing whether an AI tool worked — so he built an open-source benchmarking tool and shared it on LinkedIn. He's not a CS major. He described what he needed, and AI helped him build it.

Part 2

What CTE Teachers
Can Do Right Now

Three Things to Try

  • Teach students to be reviewers, not just users
    Have students peer review AI-generated work — a business plan, a marketing brief, a financial projection. They learn more by finding AI's mistakes than by doing it from scratch.
  • Have students build something
    A feedback form, a calculator, a marketing mockup — built with AI. Teaches requirements, iteration, and quality control.
  • Create AI portfolios
    Students collect their best AI-assisted work — prompts, outputs, and reflections on what they changed. Shows employers they can direct AI, not just use it.

Where This Leads

Skill Framework for Gies Business Learners

AI Literacy

  • Explain fundamental AI concepts and technologies
  • Recognize AI applications across business, society, and personal domains
  • Understand ethical considerations
  • Critically evaluate AI outputs and limitations

AI Competency

  • Use AI tools for business workflows
  • Solve business problems using AI-powered resources
  • Interpret AI-generated insights for business context
  • Collaborate with technical specialists on AI projects

AI Expertise

  • Develop and deploy AI solutions
  • Lead AI implementation projects
  • Strategic thinking about AI's transformative potential
  • Innovate new AI applications or frameworks
